Curator's Preface

Just as the great houses of Europe established their dominion through lineage, virtue, and divine right, so too does the Kingdom of Fruit possess its own noble hierarchy. This exhibition presents, for the enlightenment of the public, the irrefutable evidence establishing the Mangifera indica as sovereign, and the proper stations of her royal issue.

Her Majesty The Queen

Official Royal Portrait

Her Royal Highness

The Mango

"Regina Fructuum" · Queen of All Fruits

  • Reign: Over 4,000 years of documented cultivation
  • Dominion: Indigenous to South Asia; beloved across six continents
  • Royal Lineage: Over 500 noble varieties
  • Divine Favour: Sacred in Hindu tradition; offered to deities

Evidence of Sovereignty

The Mango's claim to the throne is earned through unparalleled nutritional virtue. A single fruit provides 75% of daily Vitamin C, contains 25 different carotenoids, and delivers Vitamins A, B6, and folate in quantities no other fruit can match.

"The mango is called the King of Fruits due to its rich history, unmatched taste, high nutritional value, cultural significance, and worldwide popularity." — Agricultural Historical Record

His Royal Highness The Prince

Crown Prince of the Realm

The Watermelon

"Princeps Magnus" · The Great Prince

  • Lineage: 4,000+ years; depicted in Egyptian royal tombs
  • Stature: The largest of all fruits—commanding presence
  • Virtue: 92% water; life-giving sustainer of desert kingdoms
  • Heraldry: Green and red; colours of vitality and passion

The Case for Succession

The Watermelon stands as heir apparent through demonstrated capability to sustain life. In the Kalahari Desert, the wild tsamma melon sustained the San people for six weeks at a time.

"The fruits are used by the San people for both water and nourishment, allowing survival for six weeks. A symbol of fertility, abundance and vitality." — Botanical Record

Her Royal Highness The Princess

Princess of the Blood Royal

The Raspberry

"Rubus Idaeus" · Discovered by the Gods Themselves

  • Divine Origin: Named for Mount Ida, where Greek Gods discovered her
  • Victorian Virtue: Symbol of perfection, sweetness, and modesty
  • Medicinal Grace: Healing properties documented since 1597
  • Roman Cultivation: Tended in gardens since the 4th century

The Princess's Virtue

The Raspberry governs through grace and healing virtue. The Victorians understood her nature: perfection, sweetness of character, and modesty—the very qualities expected of a princess of proper breeding.

"By the Victorian period, the raspberry symbolized perfection, sweetness of character, and modesty. The Greek Gods discovered it on Mount Ida." — Historical & Medicinal Records

Natural Child of Unknown Provenance

The Blueberry

"Vaccinium" · The Colonial Discovery

  • Origin: Wild-born in the Americas; unknown to European courts
  • Legitimacy: No cultivation until 1911—grew without tending
  • Discovery: Found by colonists, not royal botanists
  • Status: 10,000 years wild before domestication

The Question of Legitimacy

While true Royals trace their lineage through gardens of Asia and Rome, the Blueberry emerged from the untamed wilderness of the New World— unknown to civilized horticulture until mere centuries ago.

For 10,000 years, it grew wild, without cultivation, without recognition. It was not until 1911 that the blueberry was first cultivated— well after the throne was established and succession secured.

"Blueberries are native to North America. The first cultivation can be traced to Native Americans, who simply burned fields to increase yield." — Smithsonian Libraries
