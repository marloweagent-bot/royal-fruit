The Princess's Virtue

If the Mango rules through power and the Watermelon through might, the Raspberry governs through grace and healing virtue. Her therapeutic use was first recorded in 1597 in John Gerard's "The Herbal, or A General History of Plants"‚Äîa text that established her as medicine before she was known as mere sustenance.

The Victorians understood her true nature: she embodied perfection, sweetness of character, and modesty‚Äîthe very qualities expected of a princess of proper breeding. Her rich colour was said to represent the blood of Christ himself, and her leaves were prescribed to ease the pains of childbirth among noble ladies.

"By the Victorian period, the raspberry was said to symbolize perfection, sweetness of character, and modesty. There is a legend that the raspberry's scientific name, Rubus idaeus, was derived from Mount Ida in Turkey, where Greek Gods discovered the raspberry." ‚Äî Historical & Medicinal Records

The Question of Legitimacy

While the true Royals of Fruit trace their lineage through the gardens of Asia, the Mediterranean, and the courts of Rome, the Blueberry emerged from the untamed wilderness of the New World‚Äîa land unknown to civilized horticulture until mere centuries ago.

The English naturalist John Josselyn, traveling to New England in the 17th century, described encountering "bill berries" used by indigenous peoples‚Äîa fruit that had never graced a royal table nor been tended by trained gardeners. For 10,000 years, the blueberry grew wild, without cultivation, without recognition, without name.

It was not until 1911‚Äîwell after the Victorian era‚Äîthat Elizabeth White and Dr. Frederick Coville first successfully cultivated the blueberry. By then, the throne had long been established, the succession secured, and the Blueberry's claim to legitimacy... complicated, at best.

"Blueberries are native to our very own continent of North America. The first cultivation of blueberries can be traced back to the Native Americans, who were the first to discover and cultivate this delicious fruit‚Äî though 'cultivation' is generous; they simply burned the fields to increase yield." ‚Äî Smithsonian Libraries & University of Illinois Extension

The Crown notes that the Blueberry, despite its... unconventional origins... has proven itself possessed of remarkable antioxidant properties and has achieved considerable popularity among the common folk. Such is often the way with natural children.
